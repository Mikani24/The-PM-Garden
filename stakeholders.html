Stakeholders

Identifying, engaging, and managing expectations

Every project exists within a web of people who are affected by or can influence its outcome. Stakeholder management is about understanding who they are, what they care about, and how to keep them engaged. Getting this right can make or break your project.
Key concept
The power/interest grid

Categorise stakeholders by power over the project and interest in it. This tells you who to manage closely, keep satisfied, keep informed, or monitor (Mendelow, 1991).

1

Identifying stakeholders

Ask: who is affected? Who has influence? Who approves decisions? Who uses the deliverables? Don't forget less obvious stakeholders: neighbouring residents, regulators, competitors.

The four quadrants

High power, high interest

Manage closely. Sponsor, primary client. Regular detailed communication.

High power, low interest

Keep satisfied. Executives, regulators. Brief at milestones.

Low power, high interest

Keep informed. End users, community. Can be advocates.

Low power, low interest

Monitor. Don't ignore, positions can change.

Engagement strategies

!
Pro tip
For resistant stakeholders, invest in understanding their concerns. Often resistance stems from a legitimate worry. A genuine conversation can transform opponents into advocates (Bourne, 2009).
4

Managing expectations

Set expectations from the start. Be explicit about scope, timeline, assumptions.
Put it in writing. Get stakeholder sign-off on scope documents.
Communicate changes promptly. Stakeholders handle bad news, not surprises.
Flag issues early. The sooner you raise a problem, the more options you have.
